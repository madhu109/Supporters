BNS Section - 9

Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023

Home BNC Sections BNC SECTION 9

Limit of punishment of offence made up of several offences.

(1) Where anything which is an offence is made up of parts, any of which parts is itself an offence, the offender shall not be punished with the punishment of more than one of such his offences, unless it be so expressly provided.
(2) (a) Where anything is an offence falling within two or more separate definitions of any law in force for the time being by which offences are defined or punished; or
(b) Where several acts, of which one or more than one would by itself or themselves constitute an offence, constitute, when combined, a different offence, the offender shall not be punished with a more severe punishment than the Court which tries him could award for any one of such offences.

Illustrations

(a) A gives Z fifty strokes with a stick. Here A may have committed the offence of voluntarily causing hurt to Z by the whole beating, and also by each of the blows which make up the whole beating. If A were liable to punishment for every blow, he might be imprisoned for fifty years, one for each blow. But he is liable only to one punishment for the whole beating.
(b) But, if, while A is beating Z, Y interferes, and A intentionally strikes Y, here, as the blow given to Y is no part of the act whereby A voluntarily causes hurt to Z, A is liable to one punishment for voluntarily causing hurt to Z, and to another for the blow given to Y.

Example:
Vijay was caught stealing cash from the shop he worked at. In the CCTV cameras it was seen that he continued stealing for a month. He was proven gulty and a combined punishment for all the thefts was given. 

Key Points in BNS-9

Key Points of BNS-09

BNS-09 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 addresses the situation where an act constitutes multiple offences or when different parts of an act are themselves offences. It ensures that the offender is not subjected to excessive punishment in such cases. The section aims to prevent an offender from being punished multiple times for what is essentially a single criminal act, unless specifically allowed by law.

1. Offences Made Up of Multiple Parts (BNS-09 (1)):
 

  • Key Principle: When a crime is composed of different parts, and any one of those parts is also considered an offence by itself, the offender cannot be punished separately for each part unless the law explicitly states otherwise.
     

  • Example: If someone commits robbery (which includes theft and violence), they will generally receive punishment for the robbery as a whole, rather than separate punishments for theft and violence, unless the law says otherwise.
     

2. Offences Covered Under Multiple Definitions (BNS-09 (2)(a)):
 

  • Key Principle: When a single act violates multiple legal provisions or falls under more than one legal definition of an offence, the offender should not be punished more severely than the maximum punishment allowed for one of the offences.
     

  • Example: If an individual commits an act that can be defined as both theft and breach of trust, the Court will impose punishment based on the maximum sentence allowed for one of those offences, not both.
     

3. Combination of Acts Leading to a Different Offence (BNS-09 (2)(b)):
 

  • Key Principle: If several actions, which by themselves are considered offences, when combined result in a new or different offence, the punishment for the offender shall not exceed the maximum penalty for any one of the individual offences.
     

  • Example: Suppose a person commits trespass and theft. When combined, these may form the offence of burglary, but the Court will impose a sentence based on the punishment of either trespass or theft, whichever is greater, but not more than that.
     

4. No Double Punishment for Related Offences:
 

  • Important Clause: BNS-09 ensures that an offender is not subjected to "double jeopardy" or punished twice for the same set of actions unless the law specifically requires multiple punishments.
     

  • This principle is based on the idea of fairness and proportionality, preventing excessive punishment for what amounts to one criminal incident.
     

Key Definitions:
 

  • Offence: An act or omission that violates the law and is punishable by it.
     

  • Punishment: The penalty imposed by a Court for committing an offence. It can range from fines to imprisonment, death, or community service.
     

  • Multiple Definitions of Offence: An act that fits the description of two or more different criminal offences under various sections of the law.
     

  • Combination of Offences: When multiple acts, which could each be offences by themselves, combine to create a different or more severe offence.


Practical Implications:
 

  • Protection from Excessive Punishment: This provision prevents an offender from being punished multiple times for essentially the same wrongdoing, unless expressly stated otherwise.
     

  • Judicial Discretion: The Court has the flexibility to determine the appropriate punishment by choosing the offence that carries the most suitable or severe penalty, while ensuring that the punishment does not exceed what is legally allowed.
     

  • Clarification of Legal Provisions: It helps ensure clarity in cases where the same act might fall under different legal sections or involve multiple legal definitions. The offender is shielded from being punished repeatedly for essentially one crime.


Conclusion:

BNS-09 safeguards individuals from excessive or unfair punishment in cases where a single act could be classified as multiple offences or where different parts of the act are considered offences. It reinforces the principles of fairness, proportionality, and judicial discretion by ensuring that the offender is not punished more than once for the same set of actions unless expressly permitted by law. This provision is crucial in maintaining justice and preventing unnecessary harshness in sentencing.

BNS Section - 126

Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023

Home BNC Sections BNC SECTION 126

Wrongful restraint

(1) Whoever voluntarily obstructs any person so as to prevent that person from proceeding in any direction in which that person has a right to proceed, is said wrongfully to restrain that person.

Exception
The obstruction of a private way over land or water which a person in good faith believes himself to have a lawful right to obstruct, is not an offence within the meaning of this section.

Illustrations

A obstructs a path along which Z has a right to pass, A not believing in good faith that he has a right to stop the path. Z is thereby prevented from passing. A wrongfully restrains Z.

(2)Whoever wrongfully restrains any person shall be punished with simple imprisonment for a term which may extend to one month, or with fine which may extend to five thousand rupees, or with both.

Example:
Rakesh prevents Vijay from entering the metro station by blocking the main gate of the street leading up to the metro station. Rakesh has committed the offence of wrongful restraint.

Key Points in BNS-126

  1. Definition of Wrongful Restraint:
     

    • Wrongful Restraint refers to the intentional prevention of a person’s movement in any direction where that person has the legal right to proceed. It involves the obstruction of another person’s free movement, which results in the restricted individual being unable to move freely in the direction they want.
       

    • Key Elements:
       

      • Voluntary Act: The obstruction must be done voluntarily, meaning the person restraining must intend to block or prevent another from moving.
         

      • Right to Proceed: The person being restrained must have the legal right to proceed in that direction. If they do not have the legal right, the restraint may not be considered wrongful under this provision.
         

      • Direction: The restraint can occur in any direction—forward, backward, or sideways.
         

  2. Punishment for Wrongful Restraint:
     

    • The punishment for wrongfully restraining someone depends on the severity of the offense:
       

      • Simple Imprisonment: The person who commits wrongful restraint can face simple imprisonment for a term that may extend up to one month.
         

      • Fine: The offender may also be subjected to a fine, which can go up to 5,000.
         

      • Both: In some cases, the court may impose both imprisonment and fine, depending on the circumstances of the offense.
         

  3. Understanding the Offense:
     

    • Wrongful restraint is a preventive measure against the infringement of a person’s right to free movement. It does not necessarily require physical harm or contact but simply involves obstructing someone’s ability to proceed where they have the legal right.
       

    • Examples of wrongful restraint might include:
       

      • Blocking a road or doorway to stop someone from passing.
         

      • Physically standing in front of a person to prevent them from moving forward.
         

  4. Legal Implications:
     

    • The offense is considered relatively minor, reflected by the relatively short period of imprisonment (up to one month) or the imposition of a fine. However, it is still a violation of personal liberty, and thus, the law seeks to protect individuals from such unauthorized restrictions on movement.
       

  5. Difference from Wrongful Confinement:
     

    • Wrongful restraint should not be confused with wrongful confinement (defined in a different section of law). Wrongful confinement involves confining a person within specific limits, whereas wrongful restraint only involves preventing movement in one direction.
       

These definitions establish the foundational meanings of various terms used throughout the Sanhita, providing clarity for legal interpretation.
